Retrospective of My Academic & Research Journey

This page provides a personal look back at the key concepts and experiences that have shaped my path as a researcher.

Key Concepts Applied in My Research

  • Many-body perturbation theory & approximation techniques: Studied during my undergraduate thesis to understand quantum mechanical solutions and the need for real-space methods over reciprocal-space for molecular systems.
  • Molecular orbital theory & density functional theory: Learned fundamentals (including ligand-field theory) to analyze low-dimensional molecular systems and defend my thesis work.
  • Molecular electronic properties & transitions: Applied in my first publication to interpret NMR chemical shifts, Land√© g-factor variations, and related spectroscopic data.
  • Charge analysis methods (Mulliken, L√∂wdin, ELF): Used to investigate carrier localization, its limitations, and exchange/superexchange dynamics in doped systems.
  • Group theory (field splitting, Kramers degeneracy): Applied to explain orbital quenching in 3D crystals and 2D materials, and to explore how reduced dimensionality can overcome crystal field limitations.
  • Noncentrosymmetric molecular behavior: Studied to better interpret optoelectronic responses in reduced-symmetry systems.

Reflections & Lessons Learned

  • Undergraduate Research: I learned that real-world research is far more iterative and complex than textbook problems. The most valuable lesson was understanding the limitations of computational methods and the importance of method validation.
  • Collaboration: Working with peers and advisors taught me the power of diverse perspectives. My first publication was a testament to effective teamwork and communication, as we combined different expertise to solve a challenging problem.
  • Adaptability: My journey has involved shifting from theory-heavy coursework to practical, hands-on computational physics. This taught me to be adaptable and to embrace new learning opportunities outside my primary discipline.
